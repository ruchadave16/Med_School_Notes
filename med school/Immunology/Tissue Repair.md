	- [[Skin]] and [[Large Intestine]] / [[Small Intestine]] – tissue stem cells
2) [[Connective tissue]] deposition
#### Connective Tissue Deposition
- Granulation tissue replaced by [[collagen]] forming scar

**Scar Formation**
1) [[Acute Inflammation|Acute inflammation]] – [[Neutrophils]] and then [[Macrophage|macrophages]]
	- Macrophages involved with [[phagocytosis]] of microbes and dead tissue

2) Cell [[proliferation]] – cells migrate to close wound
	- Epithelial cells move over wound to close it
	- Endothelial form new blood vessels ([[angiogenesis]])
	- [[Fibroblasts]] lay down [[collagen]] to form scar
		- Cytokines from macrophages cause fibroblast proliferation
	- [[Macrophage|Macrophages]] release **TGF-$\beta$** for 
		- Fibroblast migration
		- ↑ [[collagen synthesis]]
		- Inhibition of metalloproteinases in [[Extracellular Matrix|ECM]]
		- ↓ lymphocyte proliferation
	- [[Thrombocytes|Platelets]] secrete **PDGF** for angiogenesis and wound healing

>[!Info]
>Tensile strength from Type 1 [[collagen]]
#### Connective Tissue Remodeling
- Collagen degraded through MMPs

#### Factors That Impact
- Infection – causes ↑ inflammation so slower healing
- [[Diabetes]] – multifactorial
- Nutrition status – protein and [[vitamin C]] deficiencies impair collagen synthesis
- [[Glucocorticoids]] – Steroids have anti-inflammatory effects and can interfere with scar formation
#### Abnormalities
**Chronic Wounds**
- Venous leg ulcers – chronic venous HTN
- Arterial ulcers – PVD
- Diabetic ulcers – [[diabetes]], common in lower extremities
- Pressure ulcers – pressure against tissue from bed
- Dehiscence – reopening of surgical wound

**Excess Scarring**
- Hypertrophic scar – ↑ collagen accumulation causing raised scar but regresses
- Keloid – scar grows beyond boundaries and does NOT regress
