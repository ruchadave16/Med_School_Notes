Used for [[Schizophrenia|schizophrenia]], [[Bipolar I|bipolar I]] and [[Bipolar II]], and [[psychosis]] from [[dementia]]
#### Classification
1) [[First Generation Antipsychotics]] (FGAs)
	- “typical”, “classical”
	- Chlorpromazine, haloperidol, thioridazine
2) [[Second Generation Antipsychotics]] (SGAs)
	- “atypical”
	- [[Clozapine]], olanzapine, [[aripiprazole]], quetiapine, risperidone, ziprasidone
	- Lower extrapyramidal symptoms, lower risk of hyperprolactinemia, can manage negative symptoms

>[!Note] **How to Choose**
>Consider adverse effects
>Atypical antipsychotics may help manage negative effects ← generally preferred
#### Pharmacokinetics
- Highly lipophilic so prolonged effect
- Prolonged D2 receptor occupancy → prolonged effect
	- Full relapse after discontinuation may take up to 6 months
- Metabolism = [[Cytochrome P450s|cytochrome P450s]] ([[CYP2D6]], [[CYP1A2]], [[CYP3A4]])
#### Mechanism
- Majority block [[D2 receptors]] in [[Reward Pathway|Mesolimbic pathway]]
	- Antagonism of D1 NOT associated with antipsychotic
- [[5-HT2A Receptors|5-HT2A receptor]] antagonism for atypical antipsychotics
- Others interact with [[NMDA Receptors|NMDA receptors]]
#### Adverse Effects
- [[D2 receptors]]: [[extrapyramidal symptoms]] + [[hyperprolactinemia]], dystonias
- [[5-HT2A Receptors|5-HT2A]]: **sedation, weight gain, hyperlipidemia, hyperglycemia**
- [[H1 receptor]]: sedation, weight gain, hyperlipidemia, hyperglycemia
- M1 receptor: anticholinergic (no accomodation, dry eye, mouth, difficulty urinating, blurry vision, constipation), sometimes toxic confusional state
- $\alpha1$ receptor: orthostatic hypotension, failure to ejaculate

>[!Warning]
>[[Clozapine]] = rapid release; NEVER discontinue rapidly
