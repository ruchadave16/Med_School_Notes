### Etiology
- Genetic + environmental
- Brain change early in live w manifestation later
### Pathophysiology
- No abnormalities in anatomy, brains are smaller consistently
- Certain regions smaller with entanglement of ventricles
- Dopamine hypothesis
	- Excessive [[dopamine]] activity causes
	- Support: many antipsychotic [[D2 blockers]], and dopamine boosting drugs worsen psychosis
	- Limitation: atypical antipsychotics that have less impact on D2 still work
- Serotonin hypothesis
	- Dysregulation of serotonin ([[5-HT2A receptors]])
	- Support: LSD type hallucinogens act on these receptors
		- [[Second Generation Antipsychotics|Atypical antipsychotics]] are inverse agonists of 5-HT2A
		- These receptors modulate neurotransmitter release
- Glutamate hypothesis
	- [[NMDA Receptors]] dysregulation
	- Support: [[NMDA Antagonists]] produce psychotic symptoms
		- Increase dopamine in mesolimbic 
#### Stages
1) Prodromal Phase
	- Insidious onset
	- Subtle changes in behavior and functioning
	- Social withdrawal
	- Blunted or flat affect
	- Avolition (loss of motivation)
	- Decline in academic or occupational performance
2) Active Phase
	- Prominent psychotic symptoms:
	- Hallucinations (usually auditory)
	- Delusions (persecutory, grandiose, etc.)
	- Disorganized speech and thought
	- Disorganized or bizarre behavior
	- Severe impairment in daily functioning

 3) Residual Phase
	- Reduction or absence of active psychotic symptoms
	- Persistent negative symptoms:
	- Flat affect
	- Avolition
	- Anhedonia
	- Social withdrawal
	- Possible mild positive symptoms
### Clinical Features
- 1st episode 18-25 ys men, 21-30 yrs women
- ≥ 2 symptoms for ≥1 month
- ≥ 6 month of disturbances
- Common symptoms
	- Grandiosity
	- Nihilistic
	- Paranoid
	- Somatic
	- Hallucinations – auditory, visual, olfactory, tactile
	- Disorganization
	- Negative symptoms

### Treatment
- Medication critical ([[antipsychotics]])
- Multimodal method most useful with social workers, occupational therapists, psychosocial therapy
### Complications
- BIG RISK OF **SUICIDE**
	- **50% of people with schizophrenia will attempt suicide, 5-10% will complete**
	- No medication associated with 37x increase in death by suicide and 12x increase in mortality
- Elevated risk of violence towards others
- Elevated risk of incarceration – commonly for minor crimes
- High substance abuse**** COMMON CAUSE OF DEATH
- Progressive decline in function
