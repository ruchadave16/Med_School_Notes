- R group decides antibacterial spectrum, resistance to b-lactams, and stability in acidic environment
### Classifications
1) Natural Penicillins
	- Ex: Penicillin G, Penicillin V
	- Work against [[gram positive]] ([[Streptococcus Gallolyticus]], some [[enterococcus]], and non-beta-lactamase-producing staphylococcus)
	- Susceptible to [[b-lactamase]] -> do NOT WORK against staph aureus
	- Penicillin G
		- Acid labile, given IV / IM
	- Penicillin V
		- Acid stable, given oral

2) Antistaphylococcus Penicillins
	- Ex: Nafcillin, oxacillin, dicloxacillin
	- Resistant to staphylococcal [[b-lactamase]] , work against [[MSSA]] (methicillin-sensitive staph aureus)
	- NOT WORKING against MRSA
	- Eliminated through kidney + liver

3) Aminopenicillins
	- Ex: Ampicillin (w or w/o food), Amoxicillin (give on empty stomach)
	- Expanded [[gram negative]] coverage
	- Susceptible to b-lactamase so combine with [[b-lactamase inhibitors]]

4) Ureidopenicillins
	- Piperacillin (with tazobactam)
	- Works against [[Pseudomonas aeruginosa]] and [[Enterobacters]]
	- Susceptible to b-lactamase so use [[b-lactamase inhibitors]]
	- **Broadest spectrum of penicillins**
### Pharmacokinetics
- Widely distributed EXCEPT to brain, prostate, intraocular fluids
- Mainly eliminated by [[kidneys]] so [[renal failure]] requires adjustment of dose
	- Exception: Nafcillin (biliary excretion), oxacillin (renal and biliary)

### Adverse Effects
1) Hypersensitivity Reaction
	- [[Type I Hypersensitivity]]: [[anaphylaxis]], rash, angioedema, Stevens-Johnson Syndrome
	- Cross-reaction with other b-lactams
		- [[Cephalosporins]] (~2%)
		- [[Carbapenems]] (~1%)
		- Monobactams (None)
2) Secondary Infection
	- Vaginal yeast infections
	- Clostridioides difficile colitis
3) CNS Toxicity at high IV dose
	- Seizures
	- Platelet dysfunction
4) Hematologic Toxicity
	- [[Hemolytic anemia]]
	- [[Thrombocytopenia]]
	- [[Neutropenia]]
